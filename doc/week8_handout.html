HPG 6103 • Epidemiological Methods I

Sources of Public Health Data

Lecture 8 Companion Handout

01

Why This Lecture Matters

In previous weeks you’ve worked on defining populations, study designs, and power calculations. All of that assumes that data already exist and are trustworthy. Today we ask: Where do these numbers actually come from?

As an MSc student, never accept a number without asking:

  • What data source produced this figure?
  • Who was counted – and who was left out?
  • Is this source appropriate for the question being answered?
02

The Data Ecosystem: The Funnel

1. Reality

People living their lives, getting sick, seeking care (or not). The full, messy truth we never observe perfectly.

2. Records

Registers, EMRs, survey questionnaires. Only a fraction of reality gets written down.

3. Databases

KHIS/DHIS2, KDHS datasets. Data are cleaned, coded, and aggregated.

4. Global Models & Policy

WHO/GBD estimates used to allocate budgets. Every number here has survived multiple layers of filtering.

03

Main Types of Public Health Data

3.1 Routine Health Facility Data (e.g., KHIS)

Data generated during everyday care (Registers, EMRs). Aggregated into KHIS/DHIS2.

Strengths
  • Continuous, high-frequency (monthly).
  • Good for trends and logistics.
  • Granular (facility/sub-county level).
Limitations
  • Selection Bias: Only represents service users.
  • Vulnerable to reporting rate changes.
  • Data quality issues (completeness).

3.2 Population-Based Surveys (e.g., KDHS, STEPs)

Carefully designed probability samples (e.g., KNBS Census frame) to represent the whole population.

Strengths
  • Gold standard for prevalence.
  • Captures those who don't seek care.
  • Detailed risk factor data.
Limitations
  • Expensive and infrequent (every 5 years).
  • Poor for outbreaks/real-time monitoring.
  • Not good for small geographic areas.

3.3 Civil Registration (CRVS)

Goal: Capture every birth and death with medically certified causes.
Reality in LMICs: Incomplete coverage. Home deaths often missed or coded as "old age." Essential for mortality trends, but often requires statistical adjustment.

3.4 Surveillance & Registries

IDSR: Speed over completeness. Used for outbreak detection (cholera, measles).

Registries (Cancer/Trauma): Detailed longitudinal data on specific conditions. High quality but often limited scope.

HDSS (Kilifi/Nairobi): "Demographic laboratories" following defined populations over decades.

3.5 Global Models (WHO, GBD)

They compile country data, clean it, and model missing values. Great for cross-country comparisons and macro-trends, but remember: many figures are estimates, not direct observations.

04

Data Quality: Common Villains

  • Completeness Are facilities missing? Are registers blank? Under-reporting is common.
  • Accuracy Misclassification? Age "heaping" (rounding to 30, 40)? Guessed diagnoses?
  • Timeliness How old is the data? Is it reported fast enough for outbreak response?
  • Consistency Did definitions change? A sudden "spike" might just be a new reporting tool.
05

Choosing a Source: Case Studies

Example 1: Hypertension in Kiambu

"What is the prevalence of hypertension among adults?"

Bad Source: KHIS

Clinic counts only capture those who seek care (Selection Bias). Misses the undiagnosed.

Best Source: STEPs Survey

Active screening of a random sample captures the true burden.

Example 2: Maternal Mortality Trends

"Has maternal mortality decreased in Kenya over the last 10 years?"

Challenge

Improved reporting systems can make mortality look like it's rising (Artifact).

Strategy: Triangulation

Compare CRVS, KDHS estimates, and WHO models. Look for convergence.

06

Reflection & Practice

Matching Exercise

For each question, suggested the most appropriate data source:

  • A "What proportion of children are fully immunised?" (KDHS)
  • B "Did free maternity services increase facility deliveries?" (KHIS - Routine)
  • C "What is the incidence of cervical cancer in Nairobi?" (Cancer Registry)
  • D "How does Kenya's stroke mortality compare to Tanzania's?" (GBD/WHO Model)

Take-Home Messages

  • Numbers in public health are not neutral facts; they are products of specific systems.
  • Always ask: "According to which data source?"
  • Match source → question → level of decision.
  • No single source is perfect; triangulation is powerful.
  • Protecting data quality is a core professional responsibility for epidemiologists.
