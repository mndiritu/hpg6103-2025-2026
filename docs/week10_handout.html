1. Key Definitions

Outbreak

The occurrence of cases of disease in excess of what would normally be expected in a defined community, geographical area, or season.

Cluster

An aggregation of cases grouped in place and time that are suspected to be greater than the number expected, even though the expected number may not be known.

2. Steps of Investigation

  1. Prepare for field work: Assemble team, logistics, PPE, and tools.
  2. Establish existence: Compare observed vs. expected cases. Rule out reporting artifacts.
  3. Verify diagnosis: Review clinical findings and secure laboratory confirmation (for a subset).
  4. Define a case: Create a working case definition (Time, Place, Person, Clinical).
  5. Find cases & Line list: Active search; record details in a standardized grid.
  6. Describe (Descriptive Epi): Analyze by Person (who?), Place (map), and Time (epi curve).
  7. Develop Hypotheses: Based on the descriptive findings.
  8. Test Hypotheses: Analytical epidemiology (Cohort or Case-Control).
  9. Implement Control: Stop the chain of transmission.
  10. Communicate: Reports and community engagement.

3. Interpreting Epi Curves

Pattern Appearance Meaning
Point Source Sharp rise, distinct peak, rapid fall. Single, brief exposure (e.g., food poisoning at a picnic).
Continuous Source Rise to a plateau; no distinct peak. Exposure is ongoing (e.g., contaminated city water).
Propagated Series of progressively taller peaks. Person-to-person spread (e.g., Measles, Flu). Peaks are one incubation period apart.

4. Analytical Study Designs

Cohort Study

When to use: The population is well-defined (e.g., guests at a wedding, students in a dorm).

Measure: Attack Rate (AR) and Risk Ratio (RR).

Formula: AR = (New Cases) / (Population at Risk)

Case-Control Study

When to use: The population is large or ill-defined (e.g., a city-wide outbreak).

Measure: Odds Ratio (OR).

Logic: Compare exposure history of Sick (Cases) vs. Not Sick (Controls).
