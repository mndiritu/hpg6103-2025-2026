Screening in Public Health Practice

A summary of core definitions, the Wilson & Jungner criteria, biases in evaluation, and the impact of prevalence on predictive values.

01

What is Screening?

Screening targets people who think they are well. This makes it powerful, but also ethically and logistically demanding compared to standard clinical care.

Term Target Population Example
Screening Apparently healthy (asymptomatic) Mass invitation for mammography
Early Diagnosis People with mild/vague symptoms Referral for a breast lump
Case-Finding Patients visiting for other reasons BP check during wound care
02

Why Do We Screen?

Key Concept: The DPCP
The Detectable Preclinical Phase is the window where disease is present but asymptomatic. Screening is only useful if this window is long enough to catch the disease before irreversible damage occurs.

  • Burden: The disease must be a significant health problem.
  • Treatment: Earlier detection must actually improve the outcome (not just shift the diagnosis date).
  • System: A "good" test is useless without the capacity to confirm and treat positives.
03

Wilson & Jungner Criteria (1968)

The classic checklist for any proposed programme:

The Condition

  • • Important health problem?
  • • Natural history understood?
  • • Recognisable latent stage?

The Test

  • • Valid (Sensitive/Specific)?
  • • Safe and acceptable?
  • • Clear policy on who is a "case"?

System & Economics

  • • Are diagnosis/treatment facilities available now?
  • • Is the cost balanced against benefits?
  • • Is screening a continuing process (not a one-off campaign)?
04

The Screening Paradox

Screening typically occurs in low-prevalence populations.

"Low Prevalence = Low Positive Predictive Value (PPV)"

Even a test with 95% specificity will generate huge numbers of false positives when applied to a general population where only 0.1% have the disease.

The Solution: Two-Step Design

  1. Screening Test: High Sensitivity, Low Cost (e.g., VIA). Catches everyone, including false alarms.
  2. Confirmatory Test: High Specificity (e.g., Biopsy). Filters out the false alarms.
05

Biases in Evaluation

Beware of "improved survival" statistics. They often reflect bias rather than true benefit.

A

Lead-Time Bias

Earlier diagnosis makes survival look longer because the "clock" starts sooner, even if the patient dies at the same time. We only prolonged the time they knew they were sick.

B

Length-Time Bias

Screening catches "slow-growing" (lazy) tumors because they stay in the body longer. Aggressive tumors appear between screens. Therefore, screening cohorts naturally have better prognosis.

C

Overdiagnosis

Detecting disease that meets pathological criteria but would never have caused symptoms or death in the patient's lifetime (e.g., some prostate or thyroid cancers). This leads to harmful overtreatment.

06

Evaluation Metrics

Process Indicators

Is the machinery working?

  • Coverage (% invited who came)
  • Follow-up (% positives treated)

Final Outcomes

Did health improve?

  • Disease-specific mortality
  • Quality of Life (QALYs)
